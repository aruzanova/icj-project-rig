Life in Our State

Texas is rich in biodiversity. However, many of its species are on a path to extinction due to habitat displacement and ecosystem collapse. Which of the thousands of threatened and endangered species in Texas are found in Travis County?

Endangered in Texas, Found in Travis County

Learn more about the birds, mammals, and reptiles identified by the Texas Parks & Wildlife as threatened and endangered species in counties across the state. I manually parsed the data to isolate species which are found in Travis County, automating the code integrated from Google Sheets.

Common nighthawk

Description: Common nighthawks nest in diverse habitats, from natural areas like dunes, prairies, and forests to urban flat gravel rooftops, though the shift to rubberized roofs has reduced suitable nesting sites.

Caption: Photo by Joel Sartore/National Geographic Photo Arc

Hoary bat

Description: Hoary bats are migratory, high-flying bats found statewide, with females migrating to Mexico in winter while males may stay in Texas year-round. They roost in forest foliage but are also found in unforested areas and deserts, often flying over water and open spaces.

Caption: Photo by Michael Durham/Minden Pictures/Newscom

Texas horned lizard

Description: This species inhabits open areas with sparse vegetation, such as grasslands, prairies, and scrub, using soil burrows or rodent burrows for shelter, typically below 6,000 feet and mainly below the pinyon-juniper zone in the Big Bend region.

Caption: Photo accredited to San Antonio Zoo

Western box turtle

Description: Western box turtles primarily inhabit grasslands, pastures, and woodlands, occasionally entering shallow water, and shelter by burrowing into soil or using existing burrows.

Caption: Photo by Jonathan Zander

Tricolored bat

Description: This species relies on forests, woodlands, riparian areas, and caves, with caves being particularly crucial.

Wilson's warbler

Description: Wilson’s Warblers rely on streamside forests and scrub for feeding during migration and use diverse habitats, from lowland thickets to high-elevation cloud forests, during the nonbreeding season.

Caption: Photo by Mick Thompson

Loggerhead shrike

Description: Loggerhead Shrikes prefer open habitats with short vegetation and scattered shrubs or low trees, including fields, pastures, orchards, and desert scrublands, often near roadsides, fence lines, or utility poles.

Caption: Photo accredited to Smithsonian's National Zoo and Conservation Biology Institute

White-faced ibis

Description: This species is found in specific counties during migration, favoring freshwater marshes, sloughs, rice fields, and occasionally brackish or saltwater habitats, with nesting in marshes, low trees, reeds, or floating mats. Its presence varies by season and is currently limited to near-coastal rookeries in hog-wallow prairies.

Caption: Photo by Peter Hamner/Macaulay Library

Learn More
Yellow-billed cuckoo

Description: In Texas, the Western Distinct Population Segment of this species breeds in riparian woodlands below 6,000 feet in the Trans Pecos region, arriving in May and wintering in South America. Threats include hydrologic changes preventing cottonwood and willow regeneration and livestock impacts on sapling trees.

Caption: Photo by Steve Creek/Steve Creek Wildlife Photography
