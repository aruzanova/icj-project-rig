Life in Our State

Texas is rich in biodiversity. However, many of its species are on a path to extinction due to habitat displacement and ecosystem collapse.

Explore the species found in Travis County and learn how conservation efforts aim to protect them.

Common nighthawk

Common nighthawks nest in diverse habitats, from natural areas like dunes, prairies, and forests to urban flat gravel rooftops, though the shift to rubberized roofs has reduced suitable nesting sites.

Learn More about Common nighthawk

Hoary bat

Hoary bats are migratory, high-flying bats found statewide, with females migrating to Mexico in winter while males may stay in Texas year-round. They roost in forest foliage but are also found in unforested areas and deserts, often flying over water and open spaces.

Learn More about Hoary bat

Texas horned lizard

This species inhabits open areas with sparse vegetation, such as grasslands, prairies, and scrub, using soil burrows or rodent burrows for shelter, typically below 6,000 feet and mainly below the pinyon-juniper zone in the Big Bend region.

Learn More about Texas horned lizard

Western box turtle

Western box turtles primarily inhabit grasslands, pastures, and woodlands, occasionally entering shallow water, and shelter by burrowing into soil or using existing burrows.

Learn More about Western box turtle

Tricolored bat

This species relies on forests, woodlands, riparian areas, and caves, with caves being particularly crucial.

Learn More about Tricolored bat

Loggerhead shrike

Loggerhead Shrikes prefer open habitats with short vegetation and scattered shrubs or low trees, including fields, pastures, orchards, and desert scrublands, often near roadsides, fence lines, or utility poles.

Learn More about Loggerhead shrike

White-faced ibis

This species is found in specific counties during migration, favoring freshwater marshes, sloughs, rice fields, and occasionally brackish or saltwater habitats, with nesting in marshes, low trees, reeds, or floating mats. Its presence varies by season and is currently limited to near-coastal rookeries in hog-wallow prairies.

Learn More about White-faced ibis

Yellow-billed cuckoo

In Texas, the Western Distinct Population Segment of this species breeds in riparian woodlands below 6,000 feet in the Trans Pecos region, arriving in May and wintering in South America. Threats include hydrologic changes preventing cottonwood and willow regeneration and livestock impacts on sapling trees.
